# Known Issues From the Prior Version of This Analysis

The earlier version of this work (built June 2026, archived elsewhere) went through a two-round self-audit that identified 20 problems. They were marked resolved in that draft, but the fixes are not all clearly reflected in the final numbers, and the draft's prose retained persuasive/emotional framing this round is explicitly trying to remove. Anyone redoing this analysis should independently avoid these specific mistakes rather than trust that the old draft already fixed them:

1. Cash flow "surplus" figures did not subtract baseline living expenses (groceries, transportation, phone, etc., roughly $3,000-$3,500/mo). Any surplus/deficit number must net this out first.
2. A 2-year age gap between two children means the first child is never in infant care at the same time as the second - the first is already in preschool by the time the second is born. "Two kids in infant care simultaneously" is not possible with a 2-year gap. Use "infant + preschool overlap" cost, not "infant x2."
3. Tax calculations must use Married Filing Jointly brackets, not single-filer brackets, for a married couple.
4. FICA should apply the Social Security wage base per person (two separate wage bases for a two-earner couple), and the Additional Medicare Tax (0.9% on combined wages above the MFJ threshold) should be included where relevant.
5. Closing costs (roughly 2-3%+ of purchase price, and potentially higher once origination, title, escrow, prepaid tax/insurance, and lender reserves are itemized) were originally omitted from the cash-needed-at-close and emergency-fund-runway figures.
6. Children's health insurance premium (added dependents on an employer plan) is a real recurring cost and should be included, not left out.
7. The prior draft did not systematically include life/disability insurance as a planning line item for a household with a mortgage and dependents - worth at least noting as an open consideration, not necessarily a fixed dollar figure.
8. Condo rental income is taxable (federal and state, offset partially by depreciation and expenses); net condo carrying cost should note this rather than presenting the pre-tax rental offset as the full picture.
9. A "stay-at-home parent" scenario should be explained clearly: if daycare cost is eliminated, all child-count columns in that scenario will show the same surplus - that's a function of the scenario, not a modeling error, and should be labeled as such rather than left to look inconsistent.
10. If a scenario models a single reduced income (job loss, single income, etc.), maxing out retirement contributions in that scenario is an unusual assumption and should be flagged as a theoretical case, not treated as equally realistic to a partial or minimal contribution case.
11. A "job loss" scenario needs a clearly defined resulting income - stating a floor income that doesn't match either partner's actual salary if they're the one who lost their job creates an internally inconsistent scenario. Define exactly whose income remains and what that number is.
12. Any ongoing savings goal beyond the emergency fund (rebuilding savings after a down payment, replacing a car, home repairs beyond the maintenance reserve, etc.) should be at least acknowledged as a factor that would reduce whatever "surplus" figure is presented - a surplus number that ignores this is incomplete.
13. Baseline living/child costs should be expected to scale at least somewhat with the number of children (food, clothing, activities), not stay flat regardless of child count.
14. Homeowners insurance costs should reflect the actual coverage level implied by the home price being modeled (a $700K-$800K home needs more dwelling coverage than a national-average estimate implies), particularly for a region with wildfire-related premium pressure.
15. A "both incomes transition to lower-paying jobs" scenario is more realistic as a staggered transition (one person's income drops before the other's does, with a gap in between) than as an instant simultaneous change - if modeled, note the transition period separately from the eventual steady state.
16. Emergency fund runway calculations should be clear about whether they assume unchanged spending or the ability to cut discretionary costs during a shortfall - these produce different numbers and both are worth showing if runway is discussed.
17. Childcare cost assumptions vary a lot by arrangement type (daycare center vs. nanny share vs. private nanny) - if only one figure is used, say clearly which arrangement it represents and that other arrangements cost meaningfully more.
18. When comparing outcomes across different child age-gap scenarios, do not assume a larger gap is strictly "better" without checking total cumulative cost over time, not just peak monthly cost - a larger gap can mean a longer total overlap window even if the peak is the same.
19. Any estimated cost (closing costs, insurance, etc.) should be checked against a real, current, sourced range rather than carried forward as an assumed round number.
20. Keep prose neutral. Avoid framing individual scenarios (job loss, a parent staying home, income disruption) with persuasive or emotionally loaded justification. State the scenario and its financial mechanics; do not argue for why the reader should worry about it.
